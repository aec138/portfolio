A new way of working

Results

Defined Marshmallow's multi-product strategic direction, established a new design operating model using AI tooling, and shipped an app prototype that became the blueprint for the company's first non-insurance product category.

The team

Timeframe 3 months
My role Staff Designer
Squad team Devs, PM, PT Researcher

The problem

There wasn't one.

When I joined the money team, Marshmallow didn't have a product for me to design. What they had was a strategic ambition to become a multi-product business. The competitive edge they'd built, using data to insure migrants that other companies wouldn't touch, was narrowing. Rivals could now do the same thing with Telematics. The question wasn't "how do we design this?" It was "what should this even be?"

My job was to find that answer.

The Result

New strategic direction for the app and multi products.

New ways of working for the product team using AI.

Research

Understanding our customers better.

Marshmallow's core customer was a migrant, typically a healthcare worker arriving from outside the EU, navigating a new country with an expected salary of around 37k, no credit footprint and limited financial support.

The researcher and I ran over 40 qualitative interviews with migrants who had arrived in the UK within the last year, people from all over the world, at different income levels, in different cities. We followed those with large-scale surveys to pressure-test what we were hearing.

What we learned

Arriving in the UK is expensive in ways people don't anticipate, and their lifestyles take dramatic changes which they didn't expect. The credit system, which as UK-born people know is so fundamental to long-term financial stability, is almost entirely opaque to newcomers and very tricky to start. You can't get credit without a credit history but you can't build a credit history without getting credit. It's a chicken and egg problem nobody explained.

Sometimes AI isn't the answer

I started using Claude for desk research during this phase, understanding how the migrant market in the UK was changing, what financial products existed, and what the competitive landscape looked like. It was genuinely useful there.

For synthesising the interviews themselves, we tried several AI tools and mostly abandoned them. Cultural nuance, humour, the way someone pauses before answering a question about money, the tools kept flattening exactly the things that mattered. We ended up doing it manually, which took longer and was absolutely the right call.

Friends and family were the main information sources for migrants, leaving them greatly unprepared.

The opportunity

Different directions

From the interviews, we mapped an opportunity tree with four directions:

  • Prepare people for the UK cost of living before they arrive
  • Help people understand and build their credit score
  • Create community between people going through the same transition
  • Provide a first credit product (a Marshmallow-branded SIM in a visa pack)

We surveyed across all four. The SIM idea was compelling but required infrastructure investment the business wasn't ready to make. The community tool was interesting but hard to monetise quickly. Cost of living preparation was valuable but harder to own as a product.

Credit score education and visibility kept coming back as the clearest, most actionable, most ownable opportunity. Migrants told us directly: they knew they needed a credit score, they had no idea what theirs was.

To one direction

We partnered with TransUnion to surface credit scores directly in the Marshmallow app, wrapped in an education layer to help people understand what they were seeing and what to do about it.

Then the paperwork hit. Internal compliance processes meant the feature was going to be delayed significantly. Which brought me to the more interesting problem.

Design

Experimenting with AI

With the direction confirmed, I turned to what the product should actually feel like.

Another designer had started creating 3D versions of the Marshforms, Marshmallow's brand characters. I riffed on that using AI to generate new variations, exploring how they could work within a financial product context. The existing app used colour and Marshforms heavily throughout, which created noise and meant promotions were getting lost. My instinct was to strip that back and use the 3D forms sparingly, to mark important moments rather than decorate every screen.

I explored this in Figma, then brought those frames into Claude to prototype with, so I could see how the direction held up in motion rather than just as static screens.

Multi pivots

While I was designing the credit score experience, I kept running into the same thought: the Marshmallow app was built entirely around insurance. Every pattern, every component, every assumption in the design system was scoped to one product type. If we were serious about becoming a multi-product business, the app itself needed to change, like now, and nobody had started that conversation yet.

So I started it.

Working with the iOS developer, I got connected to the Marshmallow design system inside Claude and connected to Fork. This meant I could prototype directly in the real environment, not a Figma approximation, but something that actually ran on the device, using real components, real tokens, semi-real data and real constraints.

Designing in Claude

The fun and the frustration

The feedback loop was instant in a way that Figma never quite is. I could see immediately where the design system was too narrow, where the visual language would break under a second product, where we'd need new tokens before we could ship anything new. I found issues in my own Telematics designs I hadn't spotted before. I made some embarrassing mistakes too, it turns out making a semi-circle in a real dev environment is not as intuitive as it sounds, and I spent longer on it than I'd like to admit.

But I also had more fun than I'd had in a while.

Shipping

What shipped (and what didn't)

From the vision prototype, I created branches, a way to strip out individual pieces we could actually ship incrementally, without waiting for the whole thing to be ready:

  • A new token for a title size, I shipped
  • Updated title hierarchy across the app, I shipped
  • Blog component updates, in progress when I left

The credit score feature was still moving through compliance. The broader app redesign was in its early stages. Then Marshmallow made a strategic decision to move the team toward high-interest loan products, which wasn't a direction I could align with. I left before I could ship more.

Lessons learned

AI changes the speed of the feedback loop

The most useful thing about working in the real environment wasn't that it made the work faster, it was that it made the consequences of decisions visible immediately. I could feel where things were wrong, not just see them. It made the action points afterwards very clear.

Go big then go small

The vision prototype was great for getting PMs and senior management engaged, but my devs were excited yet sceptical. This changed when I broke it down into branches, the first shippable slice, then the next. It made something the team could actually visualise on a roadmap level. It's a framing I'll use again.

What I enjoyed

Ambiguity is a design problem too

Going from "here's the brief" to "find us the brief" requires a different muscle. The research process here wasn't just informing a product, it was generating the strategy itself. That's a different kind of design work and it's one I find genuinely energising.
